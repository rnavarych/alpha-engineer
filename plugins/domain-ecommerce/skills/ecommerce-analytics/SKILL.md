# E-Commerce Analytics

## Conversion Funnel Tracking

### Funnel Stages
1. **Browse**: product listing or category page view.
2. **Product View**: individual product detail page view.
3. **Add to Cart**: item added to the shopping cart.
4. **Checkout Start**: checkout process initiated.
5. **Payment**: payment information submitted.
6. **Purchase**: order confirmed and payment captured.

### Implementation
- Emit a tracking event at each stage with a consistent session/user identifier.
- Use a data warehouse (BigQuery, Snowflake, Redshift) to store raw events.
- Build funnel queries that calculate drop-off rates between each stage.
- Segment funnels by traffic source, device type, customer type (new vs. returning), and time period.

### Identifying Bottlenecks
- A high browse-to-product-view drop-off indicates poor category page design or irrelevant search results.
- A high add-to-cart-to-checkout drop-off suggests shipping cost surprise, mandatory account creation, or trust issues.
- A high payment drop-off points to payment form UX problems or insufficient payment methods.

## Revenue Attribution

### Attribution Models
- **Last click**: credit the last marketing touchpoint before purchase.
- **First click**: credit the first touchpoint that brought the user.
- **Linear**: distribute credit evenly across all touchpoints.
- **Time decay**: give more credit to touchpoints closer to the conversion.
- **Data-driven**: use algorithmic models (Markov chains, Shapley values) to assign credit.

### Implementation
- Track UTM parameters (source, medium, campaign) on landing pages.
- Store touchpoints in a user journey table with timestamps.
- Join touchpoints with conversion events to calculate attributed revenue per channel.
- Use these insights to optimize marketing spend allocation.

## Cohort Analysis

### Cohort Definition
- Group customers by acquisition date (weekly or monthly cohorts).
- Track behavior over time: retention rate, cumulative revenue, order frequency.

### Metrics per Cohort
- **Retention**: percentage of customers who place a second order in subsequent periods.
- **Revenue per customer**: cumulative revenue generated by the cohort over time.
- **Repeat purchase rate**: percentage of the cohort that has ordered more than once.

### Visualization
- Use a cohort retention matrix (heatmap) to compare cohorts side by side.
- Identify trends: are newer cohorts retaining better or worse than older ones?
- Correlate cohort performance with marketing campaigns or product launches.

## Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

### Calculation Methods
- **Historical CLV**: sum of all past revenue from a customer minus acquisition and servicing costs.
- **Predictive CLV**: forecast future value using a probabilistic model (BG/NBD + Gamma-Gamma).
- **Simple formula**: CLV = Average Order Value x Purchase Frequency x Customer Lifespan.

### Application
- Determine acceptable customer acquisition cost (CAC): ensure CLV > CAC with a healthy ratio (3:1 or higher).
- Identify high-value customer segments for loyalty programs and personalized marketing.
- Allocate retention marketing budget based on predicted future value.

## RFM Segmentation

### Dimensions
- **Recency**: how recently the customer made a purchase (days since last order).
- **Frequency**: how often the customer purchases (total order count in a period).
- **Monetary**: how much the customer spends (total revenue in a period).

### Scoring
- Score each dimension from 1 to 5 (quintiles). A customer with R=5, F=5, M=5 is a top customer.
- Create segments: Champions (high R, F, M), Loyal (high F), At Risk (low R, high F/M), Lost (low R, F, M).

### Actions per Segment
- **Champions**: reward with exclusive offers, ask for reviews, enroll in referral programs.
- **At Risk**: send win-back campaigns with discounts or personalized recommendations.
- **New Customers**: nurture with onboarding emails and first-purchase incentives.
- **Lost**: attempt reactivation with aggressive offers; suppress from regular marketing to reduce costs.

## A/B Test Analysis

### Statistical Significance
- Define a significance level (typically alpha = 0.05) and minimum detectable effect before starting.
- Calculate the required sample size based on baseline conversion rate and expected lift.
- Use a two-proportion z-test or chi-squared test for conversion rate comparisons.
- Do not peek at results before reaching the required sample size (or use sequential testing methods).

### Revenue-Based Tests
- Compare average revenue per visitor (not just conversion rate) to capture order value differences.
- Use a t-test or Mann-Whitney U test for revenue comparisons.
- Watch for outlier orders that can skew results; consider winsorizing extreme values.

### Reporting
- Report results with confidence intervals, not just p-values.
- Calculate the expected annual revenue impact of the winning variant.
- Document test hypothesis, setup, duration, sample size, and outcome for organizational learning.

## Key Metrics Dashboard

### Core Metrics
- **Average Order Value (AOV)**: total revenue / number of orders.
- **Conversion Rate**: orders / sessions (or unique visitors).
- **Cart Abandonment Rate**: (carts created - orders completed) / carts created.
- **Repeat Purchase Rate**: customers with 2+ orders / total customers.
- **Revenue per Visitor (RPV)**: total revenue / unique visitors.

### Operational Metrics
- **Orders per day/week/month** with trend lines.
- **Fulfillment time**: average time from order to shipment.
- **Return rate**: returned orders / total orders.
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC)**: marketing spend / new customers acquired.

### Monitoring
- Set up real-time dashboards for daily operations (orders, revenue, stock levels).
- Configure alerts for anomalies: sudden conversion rate drops, spike in cart abandonment, payment failure rate increase.
- Review metrics weekly with stakeholders; deep-dive into monthly cohort and CLV reports.
